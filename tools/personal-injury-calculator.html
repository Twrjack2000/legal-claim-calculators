How Personal Injury Settlements Are Calculated

Personal injury settlements compensate victims for two categories of harm: economic damages (medical bills, lost wages, property damage) and non-economic damages (pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life).

The most widely used method for calculating pain and suffering is the multiplier method: your total economic damages are multiplied by a factor between 1.5× and 5× based on injury severity. Minor soft-tissue injuries typically use 1.5–2×; serious injuries requiring surgery use 2.5–3.5×; catastrophic or life-altering injuries can use 4–5× or higher.

Comparative negligence reduces your award by your percentage of fault. Most states use modified comparative negligence — if you're more than 50% at fault, you cannot recover. A few states (Alabama, Maryland, North Carolina, Virginia, and D.C.) use contributory negligence — any fault on your part bars recovery entirely.

Insurance policy limits are a major practical constraint. No matter how large your damages, recovery is capped at the at-fault party's policy limits unless they have personal assets worth pursuing. This is why having adequate underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage on your own policy matters.

Frequently Asked Questions

Car accident settlements vary enormously by injury severity. Minor soft-tissue injuries (whiplash, strains) typically settle between $5,000–$25,000. Moderate injuries involving fractures or one surgery often range from $25,000–$100,000. Serious injuries with permanent effects or multiple procedures can reach $100,000 to several million dollars. The strongest predictor of value is documented medical expense and prognosis.

Most cases settle within 3–18 months. Straightforward cases with clear liability and complete medical treatment resolve faster. Complex cases with disputed fault, multiple parties, or serious injuries can take 2–4 years. A key timing factor is reaching "maximum medical improvement" (MMI) — the point where doctors determine your condition has stabilized — before settling, so future costs can be properly documented.
Personal injury attorneys typically charge a contingency fee of 33% (one-third) if settled before a lawsuit is filed, and 40% if the case goes to trial. Some attorneys charge less for simple cases. In addition to the contingency fee, case expenses (expert witnesses, filing fees, medical records) are advanced by the attorney and reimbursed from the settlement. Even after attorney fees, represented claimants typically net more than unrepresented ones.
